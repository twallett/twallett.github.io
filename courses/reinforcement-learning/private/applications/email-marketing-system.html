Email Marketing System

Application: Email Marketing System üí°

Step 1: Define the Business Objective

The first step in designing the RL email marketing system is to define the business objective. Given the goal of maximising revenue while minimising unsubscribes, we define the following metrics:

  1. Total conversions (¬£) ‚Äî \(\sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{c=1}^{C} \text{conversions}_{t,c}\)
  2. Total unsubscriptions ‚Äî \(\sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{c=1}^{C} \text{unsubscriptions}_{t,c}\)

where \(t = 1, \ldots, T\) denotes each timestep for the duration of the experiment and \(c = 1, \ldots, C\) denotes each customer.

Step 2: Design the RL Framework

Prior to choosing the RL framework we need to address the type of environment in which the RL task falls into.

  • ‚ùå Non-associate environment is a setting that involves learning how to act in one state.

  • ‚úÖ Associate environment is a setting that involves learning how to act in multiple states.

Since each customer has a unique behavioural profile and journey stage (touchpoint), the agent must condition its actions on customer context making this an associative, or contextual problem.

Framework of Choice: Contextual Multi-Armed Bandit (CMAB)

The CMAB framework is a practical and industry tested approach (see Netflix CMAB implementation) to learn an optimal course of action \(\pi^{*}\), or policy, by associating actions \(a \in \mathcal{A}\) with context features \(\mathbf{x}_{t,a} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}\), where \(d\) is the dimension of the feature vector (amount of features).

Relevant to the email marketing system, the features demonstrated by each user \(\mathbf{x}_{t,a}\) are defined as:

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t,a} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Email opens} \in \{0,1\} \\ \text{Clicks} \in \mathbb{N} \\ \text{Complaints} \in \mathbb{N} \\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Additionally, the action space \(\mathcal{A}\) would be defined as:

\[ \mathcal{A} \gets \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \text{No email} \\ \text{Promotion email} \\ \text{Educational email} \\ \text{Product recommendation email} \\ \text{Renew insurance for a product} \end{array} \right\} \]

The reward signal \(r_{t,c}\) (for a particular timestep \(t\) and customer \(c\)) and with both business objectives (1. & 2.) being considered:

\[ r_{t,c} = \underbrace{\left(\sum_{\tau=1}^{7} \gamma^{\tau} \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{purchase}_{\tau,c}] \cdot \text{revenue}_{c}\right)}_{\text{Business Objective 1}} - \underbrace{\left(\alpha \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{unsubscribe}_{t,c}]\right)}_{\text{Business Objective 2}} \]

where \(\gamma < 1\) is a discount factor relative to the observed purchasing behavior of the user, and \(\tau\) denotes whether customer converts (purchase) up to 7 days after observing the action.

TipAdaptive Learning Ideas

Incremental reward accumulation

\[ r_{t,c} = (\underbrace{\sum_{\tau=1}^{t}}_{\text{Incremental}} \gamma^{\tau} \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{purchase}_{\tau,c}] \cdot \text{revenue}_{c}) - (\alpha \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{unsubscribe}_{t,c}]) \quad \text{where} \ t \in \{1, 2, \ldots, 7\} \]

  • The difficulty is that the CMAB would have to update on sparse purchasing signals which might lead to overfitting.

Proxy purchase probability

\[ r_{t,c} = \left(\underbrace{\hat{p}_{t,c}}_{\mathbf{NN}(\mathbf{x}_{t,a})} \cdot \text{revenue}_{c}\right) - \left(\alpha \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{unsubscribe}_{t,c}]\right) \]

  • The difficulty is that the proxy would introduce model error in the reward signal.
TipCMAB Touchpoint Proposition

The previous method considers optimizing over two different business objectives, with the assumption that the model will capture the behavior of the user at different stages of the customer lifecycle.

An alternative method would leverage a CMAB at each touchpoint. For example, for conversion-focused touchpoints - it might be best to have particular feature vectors \(\mathbf{x}_{t,a}^{\text{conv}}\) and action spaces \(\mathcal{A}^{\text{conv}}\):

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t,a}^{\text{conv}} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Email opens} \in \{0,1\} \\ \text{Clicks} \in \mathbb{N} \end{bmatrix}; \mathcal{A}^{\text{conv}} \gets \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \text{Promotion email} \\ \text{Educational email} \\ \text{Product recommendation email} \\ \text{Renew insurance for a product} \end{array} \right\} \]

\[ r_{t,c} = \underbrace{\left(\sum_{\tau=1}^{7} \gamma^{\tau} \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{purchase}_{\tau,c}] \cdot \text{revenue}_{c}\right)}_{\text{Business Objective 1}} \]

On the other hand, retention-focused touchpoints with feature vectors \(\mathbf{x}_{t,a}^{\text{ret}}\) and action spaces \(\mathcal{A}^{\text{ret}}\):

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t,a}^{\text{ret}} = \begin{bmatrix} \text{Email opens} \in \{0,1\} \\ \text{Complaints} \in \mathbb{N} \end{bmatrix}; \mathcal{A}^{\text{ret}} \gets \left\{ \begin{array}{c} \text{No email} \\ \text{Educational email} \\ \text{Renew insurance for a product} \end{array} \right\} \]

\[ r_{t,c} = - \underbrace{\left(\alpha \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{unsubscribe}_{t,c}]\right)}_{\text{Business Objective 2}} \]

Algorithm of Choice: LinUCB

LinUCB is a bandit algorithm that leverages the CMAB framework. The objective of LinUCB is to learn linear weights \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_a\) for each action \(a \in \mathcal{A}\) using a ridge estimator.

NoteLinUCB Example

To estimate \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_a\) we initialize the design matrix and reward vector for each arm \(a\) as:

\[ \mathbf{A}_a \gets \mathbf{I}_{d} \qquad \mathbf{b}_a \gets \mathbf{0}_{d} \]

import numpy as np

d = 2  # feature dimension (e.g. opens, complaints)
n_arms = 5  # no email, promotion, educational, product recommendation, renewal

A = [np.identity(d) for _ in range(n_arms)]
b = [np.zeros(d) for _ in range(n_arms)]

The parameter vector \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_a\) is estimated as the closed-form ridge regression solution:

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_a = \mathbf{A}_a^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{b}_a \]

theta = [np.linalg.solve(A[a], b[a]) for a in range(n_arms)]

The agent selects the action with the highest UCB score, balancing exploitation of known rewards with exploration of uncertain arms via \(\alpha\):

\[ p_a = \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_a^\intercal \cdot \mathbf{x}_{t,a} + \alpha \sqrt{\mathbf{x}_{t,a}^\intercal \mathbf{A}_a^{-1} \mathbf{x}_{t,a}} \]

alpha = 1.0
x = np.array([1, 0])  # example context: email opened, no complaint

scores = []
for a in range(n_arms):
    theta_a = np.linalg.solve(A[a], b[a])
    exploit = theta_a @ x
    explore = alpha * np.sqrt(x @ np.linalg.solve(A[a], x))
    scores.append(exploit + explore)

action = np.argmax(scores)

After observing reward \(r_t\) the model updates incrementally:

\[ \mathbf{A}_a \gets \mathbf{A}_a + \mathbf{x}_{t,a} \mathbf{x}_{t,a}^\intercal \qquad \mathbf{b}_a \gets \mathbf{b}_a + r_t \cdot \mathbf{x}_{t,a} \]

A[action] += np.outer(x, x)
b[action] += r * x

Why Not Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP)?

The problem with POMDPs mainly relies in the intractability of the state transition dynamics \(P(s^{'},r|s,a)\) of human behavior and latent preferences which is inherently:

  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Stochastic.
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Non-stationary.
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Confounding variables (e.g. events, mood, financial circumstances).

CMABs tackle this issue by considering each feature vector to be independent of each other.

Step 3: Evaluating RL Model Performance

Before deploying the CMAB in production, it is essential to validate its performance against a baseline through rigorous A/B testing. The baseline could be a rule-based system or the existing email marketing strategy, with the CMAB as the treatment.

  • Defining Control and Test Groups - customers are randomly split by customer ID hash to ensure consistent assignment. The control group receives the existing email strategy, the treatment group is served by the CMAB.
  • Setting Statistical Measures - a minimum detectable effect (MDE) is defined upfront based on business expectations, with power \(1 - \beta = 0.8\) and significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\).
  • Hypothesis Testing - a one-tailed t-test on revenue per customer:

\[H_0: \mu_{\text{CMAB}} \leq \mu_{\text{baseline}} \qquad H_1: \mu_{\text{CMAB}} > \mu_{\text{baseline}}\]
